Battle of Cartagena de Indias
The Battle of Cartagena de Indias took place during the 1739 to 1748 War of Jenkins' Ear between Spain and Britain. The result of long-standing commercial tensions, the war was primarily fought in the Caribbean; the British tried to capture key Spanish ports in the region, including Porto Bello and Chagres, in Panama, Havana and Cartagena de Indias, in present-day Colombia. Two previous naval attacks failed in 1740, while the third attempt in March 1741 was a combined naval and ground assault. The British were forced to retreat, having lost over 9,000 men, the majority from yellow fever; some units suffered death rates of 80-90%. Victory demonstrated Spain's ability to defend its position and largely ended active operations in this area. Both countries switched focus to the wider European War of the Austrian Succession and hostilities ended with the 1748 Treaty of Aix-la-Chapelle. Background The 1713 Treaty of Utrecht gave British merchants access to Spanish colonies or New Spain. This included the Asiento or monopoly to supply 5,000 slaves a year and the Navio de Permiso, permitting two ships a year to transport 500 tons of goods each for sale in Porto Bello and Veracruz.

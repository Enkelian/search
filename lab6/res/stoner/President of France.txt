President of France
The president of France, officially the president of the French Republic (French: président de la République française, French pronunciation: ​[pʁezidɑ̃ də la ʁepyblik fʁɑ̃sɛːz]), is the executive head of state of France in the French Fifth Republic. In French terms, the presidency is the supreme magistracy of the country. The powers, functions and duties of prior presidential offices, as well as their relation with the prime minister and Government of France, have over time differed with the various constitutional documents since the French Second Republic. The president of the French Republic is also the ex officio co-prince of Andorra, grand master of the Legion of Honour and of the National Order of Merit. The officeholder is also honorary proto-canon of the Basilica of St. John Lateran in Rome (although some have rejected the title in the past). The current president of the French Republic is Emmanuel Macron, who succeeded François Hollande on 14 May 2017. History The presidency of France was first publicly proposed during the July Revolution of 1830, when it was offered to the Marquis de Lafayette.

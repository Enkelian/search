Russian irredentism
Russian irredentism generally refers to irredentist claims to parts of the former Russian Empire or the USSR made during the 21st century for the Russian Federation. The Annexation of Crimea has been partially explained as an irredentist claim. History Russian Empire From roughly the 16th century to 20th century, the Russian Empire followed an expansionist policy. Few of these actions had irredentist justifications, though the conquest of parts of the Ottoman Empire in the Caucasus to bring Armenian Christians under the protection of the Tsar may represent one example. Post-USSR (21st century) After the Dissolution of the USSR in 1991, it was thought that the Russian Federation gave up on plans of territorial expansion or kin-state nationalism, despite some 25 million ethnic Russians living in neighboring countries outside Russia. Stephen M. Saideman and R. William Ayres assert that Russia followed a non-irredentist policy in the 1990s despite some justifications for irredentist policies - one factor disfavoring irredentism was a focus by the ruling interest in consolidating power and the economy within the territory of Russia.
